Inside the Automation Stack: How We Built a Zero-Touch Invoice Processing System

A technical deep-dive into the architecture, tooling decisions, and failure modes we encountered building a production-grade document automation pipeline that processes thousands of invoices daily with no human review.

Sofia Reyes

Invoices arrive in dozens of formats. PDFs from legacy ERP systems, scanned paper documents, email attachments with varying layouts, and increasingly, structured XML feeds from e-invoicing platforms. The client — a mid-size logistics company processing roughly 4,000 invoices per month — was spending 1.5 FTEs purely on manual data entry, reconciliation, and exception handling. They wanted to get that number to zero.

This is the story of how we built the system that got them there, the decisions that worked, and the ones that didn't.

The Problem with "Just Use OCR"

The first instinct for invoice automation is always OCR. And it works — up to a point. Off-the-shelf OCR handles clean, well-structured PDFs reasonably well. But production invoice processing has a long tail of edge cases that breaks naive OCR pipelines fast:

  • Scanned documents with skew, shadows, or low resolution
  • Table structures that span multiple pages with inconsistent headers
  • Fields in different positions depending on the vendor template
  • Hand-annotated corrections made by the client's AP team on digital PDFs

We tested three commercial OCR providers in week one. Accuracy on clean PDFs was 97%+. Accuracy on the full incoming document mix dropped to around 74%. At 4,000 invoices per month, that means roughly 1,040 documents per month still requiring manual touch — barely an improvement.

The Architecture We Landed On

We built a four-stage pipeline:

Stage 1 — Ingestion & Normalisation

All documents enter through a single intake queue regardless of source (email, upload portal, or API). A lightweight classifier routes them by document type (invoice, credit note, remittance advice) before anything else runs. This alone eliminated a significant category of downstream errors caused by running invoice-specific extraction logic on non-invoice documents.

Stage 2 — Extraction

Rather than a single OCR engine, we built an ensemble approach. A vision-language model handles layout understanding and field localisation, while a specialised table extraction model handles line items. Both run in parallel; a confidence scoring layer decides which output to trust per field. Fields below a confidence threshold are flagged for human review rather than silently passed downstream.

Stage 3 — Validation & Enrichment

Extracted data is validated against business rules (VAT number format, currency codes, payment terms within client-defined bounds) and enriched by matching vendor IDs against the client's supplier master data. Mismatches surface as exceptions with suggested corrections — not hard rejections.

Stage 4 — ERP Integration

Clean, validated records are posted directly to the client's ERP via API. Exception records enter a lightweight review UI where an AP team member can accept the AI-suggested correction with one click or override manually. Every correction feeds back into the model's confidence calibration.

What Failed (And What We Learned)

The first version of Stage 2 used a single large vision-language model for everything. It was accurate but slow — average processing time per document was 11 seconds, which was unacceptable at volume. Splitting into specialised models (layout vs. table extraction) brought average time to 2.3 seconds with equal or better accuracy.

We also initially built the exception UI as a separate application. The AP team hated the context switch. Moving exception review directly into the client's existing AP workflow — surfacing it as a task in the tool they already used — increased review throughput by 40% in the first week after deployment.

Results After 90 Days

After three months in production, the numbers looked like this:

  • 96.2% of invoices processed with zero human touch
  • 3.8% flagged for exception review (down from 100% previously)
  • Processing time reduced from 3–5 business days to under 4 hours end-to-end
  • 1.5 FTEs redeployed to higher-value reconciliation and supplier relationship work

The 3.8% exception rate is not a failure — it's a deliberate design choice. A system that flags uncertainty is more trustworthy than one that guesses silently. The AP team now spends their time on genuinely ambiguous cases, not mechanical data entry.

Closing Thoughts

Document automation gets more interesting the closer you look at the edge cases. The technology to hit 70–80% automation exists off the shelf. Getting to 95%+ requires understanding the specific failure modes in your document population and designing around them deliberately. It also requires a feedback loop — a system that learns from its own mistakes is the only kind that stays accurate as vendor templates and business rules evolve.

If you're dealing with a similar document-heavy workflow, the starting point is always an honest audit of your actual document mix — not a demo on clean test data.
